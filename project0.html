overview

The Design Report is a data visualization project based on the 2017 AIGA Design Census.

This project is technical, visual, and interactive. Not only was our team able to code the entire website, including using javascript and D3.js, we also designed a cohesive visual system able to handle quantitative content.

The data supporting this visualization includes 10,000+ data points from designers around the nation. In making the data interactive, we needed to make sure that our solution was transparent as possible, and didn't overly distort the data. Through many iterations, we settled on the treemap for the overview, for it's simplicity and proportional display.

designing the visualization

the aiga census

The design census is meant to capture a snapshot of the design industry every year, and asks questions that range from personal data such as specialty of work, skills, location, age, gender, education, sexual orientation, to more subjective opinion, such as what design skills might be more important in coming years.

How do you filter and organize over 300,000 survey responses?

With a survey that reached over 10,000 people and asked over 30 questions, our challenge was not only designing a data visualization piece, but also to come up with a concept that used the data to tell a specific story. Conceptualizing a story in this way would help us filter out which survey questions we thought were most compelling.

process

We started out by printing out the entire data set, cutting it up, and rearranging and categorizing the different survey questions. As we got a handle of on the data, we played around with how we would actually visualize it—from the beginning, we knew we wanted to create some sort of linear story while still showing the breadth and depth of the responses. We played around with different types of forms and floating UIs, as well as the technical tools (D3.js) available to us.

concept + goals

At the time of the project, all of us were college juniors, so naturally, careers and work were constant, if often stressful, conversation topics. Driven by this anticipatory air, we decided that the AIGA census would be a great resource for ourselves and our classmates to get a feel of current working designers’ education and career paths.

Main takeaway: conceptualization and storytelling are key to powerful data visualization

While the AIGA census website includes different data viz charts, we thought that the visualization they provided isolated each survey response in a way that was unrepresentative of the overall design industry landscape. Our goal was to create a more cohesive map of how designers are currently working by creating a system that allows the user to cross-filter responses so they can find exactly what they're looking for. For example, as a student who’s interested in working in graphic design at an agency, I might want to create a filter that encompasses “graphic designers working in agency for 5-9 years,” which would then return to me a further breakdown of education, skills, and salaries.
