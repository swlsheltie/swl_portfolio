airbnb design challenge

ABSTRACT

Airbnb has always been an intriguing and attractive company to me, and their product is one of my favorites. They heavily value creating trust between two potential strangers. To me, human-human problems like this are the most complex and interesting to tackle, which is one the main reasons I chose to look at Airbnb.

I ultimately designed for two main experiences: trust (through rethinking aspects of the reviews product), and exploration (an extrapolation of the experiences + homes product)

problem space

In light of the recent addition of Experiences and Restaurants in addition to Homes, how can Airbnb engage users in learning about a destination they're interested in, prior to arriving?

motivation

In planning a trip for upcoming Spring break, my friends and I found that it was often hard to find general location information (restaurants, things to do), on booking sites. There's a lot of back and forth between travel blogs and the booking site. On an obscure part of Airbnb's website, there is a wealth of information on things to do, but non-existent in app form.

the research

product analysis + interviews

personal experience

In booking stays for upcoming Spring break, I found that typically, it is very hard to find general location information (restaurants, things to do) on the app, but not impossible. However, their website, which hosts a wealth of information, is apparently non-existent in app form.

interviews

I talked to 5 people and conducted these interviews in a conversational manner, asking guided questions along the way. I also presented several hypothetical user scenarios, and used these “stories” as a foundation to examine the app.

In my interviews, a few of the key take-aways that I heard were:

1. It’s easy to get lost in the app
2. Airbnb has experiences and restaurants now, but it’s hard to navigate between them
3. Not sure if “Experiences” are worth it, or if they will be convenient
4. Photos, convenience, and knowing the overall worth of a place.

product overview

It’s clear that Airbnb is no longer just about keeping people accommodated during their stay while traveling, but also helping them get around wherever they are. With Experiences, Airbnb is empowering local hosts to show travelers around, and meet new people.

However, while these features complement together nicely and are wonderful services, they feel a bit disjointed.

Because Airbnb is increasingly marketing itself as an all encompassing travel app, I also wanted to focus on the idea of exploration.

I decided to focus on the user’s exploratory/location research process, so I could look at their end-to-end interactions.

concepting

ideation +
prototyping

At this point, I consolidated all the research that I had done, and moved towards better-defining the problems I identified. From there, I moved towards concepting, refining, and prototyping solutions.

designing for mobile

In addition to looking at their mobile app, I also extensively looked at their desktop app. While the two main products are similar, Airbnb’s website boasts a lot more information than the app. Specifically, their city guides seemed very in-depth, and contained many host reviews.

However, if City Guides are available in app/mobile form, they are not accesible.

I wanted to make this part of the exploration process available in the app. Because the website is best viewed in desktop form, I wanted to see if I could not only translate it to a mobile scale, but also integrate it into the app visual language.

persona

This is Alexandro.

He’s 22, from Maine, and just graduated college.

With school recently behind him, and a job lined up, Alex wants to travel for a bit before he has to start work. He’s not exactly sure where he wants to go yet, but he’s interested in South America or California. He has never done any research on traveling before, and isn’t sure where to start.

Alex looks for places to stay along the Pacific Coast Highway. He finds the Explore feature really useful, since it allows him to see homes, experiences, and suggestions all at once.

The Photo Tours are especially helpful, since he doesn’t have any prior knowledge of Southern California cities. With the suggestions and map exploration, he’s able to plan out several sights he wants to see in each city he’s in.

Using the Alexandro persona, I created a scenario to design a solution around.

solution

1. home page

On Airbnb’s home page, if the user searches for a city, they will be shown this “Bird’s Eye View” map, which shows relative locations of Homes, Experiences, and Restaurants.

Users can tap on the dots for a preview of what is in each location.

Tap "Explore the City" to enter the “Explore” feature

2. "Explore" feature

Explore mode is a city view that includes homes, experiences, restaurants, and even host guidebook tips.

Users can filter through different types of information. (Only Tips icons show here).

Result cards are scrollable horizontally, and are clickable for more info. (Only Parks & Nature Tips icons show here).

Host guidebooks have a wealth of information, but are hidden deep in their profiles.

Airbnb can leverage can this information to help users make decisions on where to travel.

By consolidating this information, Airbnb can provide a very in-depth, reliable view of the city, from a local’s point of view.

3. Iconography

Integrating the 3 types of services into a map means that there needs to be visual differentiation.

Resy Restaurants are denoted by the teardrop shape and the “R.”

Host Tips are rounded rectangles.

Airbnb home listings remain circular on the integrated map.

4. Further Details

These “location” pages take existing information that Airbnb already has. Users can add the location to an existing itinerary.

moving forwards

Moving forward, I would definitely take more time to focus on the visual differentiation between the three services, if they were to exist together. I would also take more time to conduct more in-depth interviews. The final feedback I received was also very thoughtful, and I’d especially like to consider how social media could play a role.

I felt challenged in new ways, and it was really interesting to focus mostly on experience design.
